AI Acceptable Use Policy: what generic templates get wrong.

You can download a free "AI acceptable use policy" in thirty seconds. The problem: most of them are a list of bans nobody reads, missing the parts that actually prevent a data leak or satisfy a customer's security review. Here's what a real one includes.

The short version: A useful AI policy isn't a wall of prohibitions — it's a short, practical set of rules that answers four questions: which tools are approved, what data must never go in, who reviews the output, and how new tools get vetted. Generic templates usually nail the bans and miss all four.

Why the free templates fall short

The typical downloaded template is a page of "do not use AI to do X." That's the easy part. What's missing is everything that makes a policy operational: a list of which tools people are actually allowed to use, a clear rule about sensitive data, a human-review expectation, and a process for the next tool someone wants to adopt. Without those, the policy doesn't change behavior — and it won't hold up when a customer's security questionnaire or an auditor asks how you govern AI.

What a real AI use policy includes

  1. Approved tools (an allowlist). Name the AI tools staff may use and for what. "Use AI responsibly" is not a control; "Copilot and ChatGPT Team are approved; consumer ChatGPT is not" is.
  2. The no-sensitive-data rule. The single most important line: never paste customer, patient, financial, or confidential data into a tool that isn't approved and contractually covered. Spell out what "sensitive" means for your business.
  3. Human-in-the-loop. AI output gets reviewed by a person before it's used in a decision, a record, or anything sent to a customer. AI assists; it doesn't get the final word.
  4. Vendor vetting & data terms. Before a new AI tool is adopted, someone checks what the vendor does with your data — training opt-out, retention, sub-processors — and whether a DPA or BAA is needed.
  5. Approval & ownership. Who approves a new AI tool, and who owns AI risk. Without an owner, shadow AI fills the vacuum.
  6. Acknowledgment. Staff sign that they've read it. An unacknowledged policy is hard to enforce and weak as evidence.

How this maps to the NIST AI RMF

These aren't arbitrary — they line up with the four functions of the NIST AI Risk Management Framework: the policy and ownership are Govern, the tool allowlist is Map, vendor/data vetting is Measure, and human review plus the approval process are Manage. A policy built this way is something you can show a customer or auditor, not just file away.

The four things templates miss

  • An allowlist of approved tools — not just a list of bans.
  • A concrete no-sensitive-data rule, defined for your business.
  • A human-review expectation for AI output.
  • A vetting + approval process for the next tool.
Honest note: a good AI policy is short — one to two pages people actually read beats a twenty-page document they don't. The goal is changed behavior and defensible evidence, not length.

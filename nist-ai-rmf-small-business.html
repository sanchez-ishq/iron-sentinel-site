Plain-English AI Governance Guide

NIST AI RMF for small businesses: what it is and why it's about to matter.

The NIST AI Risk Management Framework sounds like something only a Fortune 500 needs. It isn't. It's a practical structure for governing the AI tools your team already uses — and customers, insurers, and auditors are starting to ask whether you follow it.

The short version: The NIST AI RMF (officially NIST AI 100-1) is a voluntary framework for managing the risks of AI. It is built around four functions — Govern, Map, Measure, Manage — and you can apply a right-sized version of it in an afternoon. It is becoming the common language for "do you have your AI under control?"

What it actually is

NIST — the U.S. National Institute of Standards and Technology — publishes the security frameworks much of the business world runs on (the Cybersecurity Framework, SP 800-53, and others). In 2023 it released the AI Risk Management Framework to do for AI what those did for security: give organizations a structured, voluntary way to identify and reduce risk.

It is not a law, a certification, or a checklist you "pass." It is a way of thinking about AI risk that you can document — which is exactly what makes it useful when someone asks how you govern AI.

The four functions, in plain English

Govern

Set the rules: who owns AI decisions, what's allowed, and the policies staff follow. The foundation everything else sits on.

Map

Know what you have: an inventory of every AI tool in use and the context it operates in. You can't manage what you haven't listed.

Measure

Assess the risk: evaluate each tool's data exposure, vendor practices, and potential for harm before and after you adopt it.

Manage

Act on it: prioritize the risks, put controls and human review in place, and have a plan for when something goes wrong.

That's the whole model. For a small business, "implementing the NIST AI RMF" can be as simple as: a written AI use policy (Govern), a list of your AI tools (Map), a quick risk rating for each (Measure), and an approval + review process (Manage).

Why your auditor, insurer, and customers will ask

Three forces are converging:

  • Customers. Enterprise security questionnaires now include AI questions — "do you have an AI acceptable use policy?", "how do you vet AI vendors?" If you sell to bigger companies, you'll see these.
  • Insurers. Cyber-insurance underwriters are beginning to ask about AI governance at renewal, the same way they started asking about MFA a few years ago.
  • Regulators & auditors. Existing frameworks (HIPAA, SOC 2) increasingly expect you to account for AI tools that touch sensitive data. A SOC 2 auditor will ask how an AI vendor in your environment is managed.

None of these require the NIST AI RMF by name — but it's the framework everyone is converging on as the answer to "show me you've thought about this."

Right-sized starting point

  • Govern: write a one-page AI Acceptable Use Policy and have staff acknowledge it.
  • Map: list every AI tool in use (ChatGPT, Copilot, an AI scribe, anything embedded in your SaaS).
  • Measure: rate each for data sensitivity and vendor risk; confirm a DPA or BAA where needed.
  • Manage: require approval before new tools go live, and add AI scenarios to your incident plan.
Honest note: there is no official "NIST AI RMF certification." Anyone selling you one is selling something that doesn't exist. The value is in actually doing it and being able to show your work — not a badge.

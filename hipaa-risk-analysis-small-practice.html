HIPAA Risk Analysis: the required step most practices skip.

The Security Risk Analysis is the foundation of HIPAA Security Rule compliance — and the single most common finding in HHS settlements with small practices. Here's what it actually is, why your EHR vendor isn't doing it for you, and how a new AI scribe changes it.

The short version: If your practice creates, receives, or stores electronic protected health information (ePHI), the HIPAA Security Rule requires a documented Security Risk Analysis (SRA) — and you must keep it current. It is not a one-time form, not something your EHR vendor does for you, and not optional. When OCR investigates a small practice, "no current risk analysis" is the finding they cite first.

What a Security Risk Analysis actually is

An SRA is a documented, organization-wide assessment of the risks to the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of the ePHI you hold. In plain terms: where does patient information live, what could go wrong, how likely is it, and what are you doing about it?

It covers all of it — your EHR, email, devices, where data is stored and backed up, who has access, and every vendor that touches PHI. It's required by the Security Rule's administrative safeguards, and it has to be reviewed and updated when things change.

Three myths that get practices in trouble

  • "Our EHR vendor handles HIPAA." Your EHR vendor secures data inside their system, under their BAA with you. The SRA is an assessment of your whole practice — they don't do it for you, and your other tools aren't in their scope.
  • "We did one when we opened." An SRA from three years ago that predates your current tools is out of date. It must be updated when you add systems, vendors, or — critically — AI tools.
  • "We're too small to matter." The majority of HIPAA settlements in recent years have been with small and mid-size practices, frequently for a missing or stale risk analysis and inadequate Business Associate Agreements.

Why your AI scribe just changed your risk analysis

Ambient AI documentation tools — DAX, Nabla, Suki, Abridge — listen to or process the patient encounter. That means they handle PHI, which makes the vendor a HIPAA Business Associate. Two things follow immediately:

  • You need a signed Business Associate Agreement with the AI vendor (separate from your EHR's BAA).
  • Your Security Risk Analysis must be updated to include the new data flow, vendor, and risks the tool introduces.

Most practices adopt these tools for the time savings and never circle back to the compliance side. That gap — a new PHI-handling vendor with no BAA and an SRA that doesn't mention it — is exactly what an examiner or your malpractice carrier will look for.

What a current SRA needs to cover

  • An inventory of where ePHI is created, received, stored, and transmitted — including AI tools.
  • A signed BAA with every vendor that touches PHI (EHR, billing, AI scribe, cloud, email).
  • Threats and vulnerabilities, with a likelihood and impact rating for each.
  • The safeguards in place and a remediation plan for the gaps.
  • A review date — and an actual review when tools or vendors change.
Honest note: a risk analysis is a real piece of work, but for a small practice it's a few hours with the right structure — not a six-figure consulting project. The point is that it's documented and current, so that if anyone ever asks, you can show it.
